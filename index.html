Unlocking Housing

The biggest item on most people’s bill is housing. And yet Europe simultaneously holds an enormous stock of housing that sits empty — villages depopulated by rural exodus, islands abandoned, apartments locked in speculation. We are working to unlock it.

The thesis Follow the work

The asymmetry

Two facts sit side by side: housing is the single largest item on most people’s bill, and much of Europe’s housing stock sits unused. Villages emptied by Landflucht (rural exodus). Empty Italian villages, abandoned Greek islands, apartments held idle by speculative investment. In Styria, roughly one in seven dwellings sits empty. Across southern Europe, entire communities have been depopulating for decades.

This is not a shortage of housing. It is a shortage of the conditions that bring housing into life: trust, structure, and the right infrastructure. Once those conditions exist, what was wasted becomes sovereign.

Live where others vacation

The rural exodus left behind beautiful places with empty houses. Unlocking Housing is the work of making those places liveable again — not as holiday lets, but as communities.

Regeneration, not extraction

The lowest-carbon housing is the house that already exists. Renovating and repeopling existing stock regenerates regions, restores social fabric, and avoids new extraction from land and materials.

Multidimensional wealth

A home in a rooted community generates financial, social, cultural, and living wealth simultaneously. This is not anti-wealth — it is better wealth, held in more forms at once.

Integral, not alternative

Competent, rooted, prosperous regeneration — legible to a conservative village council and to an impact investor alike. Not a drop-out aesthetic. A serious offer.

The unlock mechanism

Empty properties do not unlock through price alone. They unlock through structured deals and emotional trust. An owner who would never sell will often enter a well-structured arrangement with people they trust. A village that resists outside buyers will welcome a Genossenschaft that restores a building and brings life back to it.

The mechanism is: find the properties, build the trust, structure the deal, renovate well, hold it in sovereign stewardship. Not buy-and-sell — buy-and-hold, as a commons. The renovation itself is part of the value: a Sanierung done properly is a good deal on its own terms, and it transforms something declining into something growing.

We are building the geo-intelligence layer that makes finding the right properties possible: which buildings in which regions have the combination of vacancy, access, bioregional quality, and planning context that makes the deal viable. The tools support the human work — they do not replace it.

Where we start

Austria and Italy are the first focus — both have significant rural vacancy, high quality of life in underused regions, and legal structures (Genossenschaft, cooperatives) suited to sovereign stewardship. The Styrian countryside, the villages of Carinthia, the Italian depopulation belt from Molise to Calabria: these are places where the asymmetry is sharpest and the intervention most legible.

From there: the night-train network is the geography. Anywhere reachable from Vienna overnight is in scope. The Netherlands as a second hub. Greece as a longer-horizon opportunity. Europe as the starting frame; the methodology as something that can travel.
